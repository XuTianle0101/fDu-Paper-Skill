# Graduate Thesis Writing Quality Checklist

Use this reference when drafting, revising, or auditing thesis prose.

## Story Before Sentences

Before polishing, write the thesis chain in one line:

`problem -> gap -> objective -> method/material -> evidence -> findings -> contribution -> limitation`

If a paragraph or chapter cannot be mapped to this chain, clarify whether it is background, method, evidence, interpretation, or administrative content.

## Paragraph Flow

- One paragraph carries one message.
- The first sentence states or frames that message.
- Each later sentence should relate by cause, contrast, consequence, example, definition, evidence, or refinement.
- Avoid paragraphs that only list papers, tools, parameters, or definitions without explaining why they matter.
- Use reverse outlining: extract each paragraph's topic sentence and check whether the sequence forms a logical outline.

## Claim-Evidence Alignment

For every important claim, mark:

- Claim: what is asserted.
- Evidence: data, source, figure, table, proof, case, citation, or official rule.
- Status: supported, partially supported, pending, or unsupported.
- Revision: keep, narrow, move, mark as pending, or delete.

Do not let the abstract or introduction claim more than the results and conclusion can support.

## Section-Level Checks

### Abstract

- Does it include problem, gap, objective, method, findings, and significance?
- Are Chinese and English abstracts structurally equivalent?
- Are numbers and terms consistent with the main text?

### Introduction

- Does the literature review end with research gaps rather than summaries only?
- Does each objective correspond to a later chapter?
- Are contribution or innovation claims evidence-bounded?

### Methods or Foundation

- Can another researcher understand the data/materials, assumptions, procedure, and evaluation criteria?
- Are standards, ethics, reliability, validity, and uncertainty addressed when relevant?

### Results and Discussion

- Are results separated from interpretation?
- Are figures/tables introduced and then interpreted?
- Are comparisons fair and tied to the same conditions?
- Are negative, null, or limited findings described honestly?

### Conclusion

- Are conclusions findings rather than activity summaries?
- Are limitations specific?
- Does the outlook follow from limitations rather than opening unrelated new projects?

## Language and Terminology

- Keep names, translated terms, abbreviations, variables, and units stable.
- Define abbreviations and symbols before first use.
- Prefer precise verbs: establish, compare, evaluate, verify, reveal, analyze, construct, synthesize, demonstrate.
- Avoid vague praise such as "significant value" or "good effect" without evidence.
- Avoid inflated novelty such as "first" unless supported by a careful literature check.
